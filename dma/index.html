Air France Multi-Platform Case Study
7 search engines · ROA · Booking probability · Four-quadrant framework
Case ▼

Strategic Context

International airline diversifying ad spend across 7 search engines. No single platform excels across all metrics — classic yin-yang trade-offs.

Core Metrics Framework

Net Revenue = Transaction Revenue − Ad Campaign Cost
ROA (Return on Ad Spend) = Net Revenue ÷ Cost
Booking Probability = CTR × Conversion Rate

Platform Trade-offs Observed

PlatformStrengthWeakness
Google USHighest net revenue ($1.4M)Highest cost — 2× competitors
MSNHighest booking probabilityOnly ~10% of Google's volume

Four-Quadrant Decision Framework

Quadrant 1 — Cut

Low booking probability + High cost → Reduce or eliminate funding

Quadrant 2 — Scale

High booking probability + Low cost → Increase funding immediately

Quadrant 3 — Optimize

High booking probability + High cost → Tactical optimization needed

Quadrant 4 — Improve

Low booking probability + Low cost → Fix performance before scaling

Diagnostic Logic

  • Low CTR → ad copy problem (creative, phone numbers, star ratings)
  • Low conversion rate → landing page issue (website experience)
  • Bubble chart visualization: booking probability (x-axis) vs. cost (y-axis); bubble size = budget

Group Exercise Instructions

  • Groups of 5–6 formed during break
  • Each group selects one engine: MSN, Google Global, or Overture
  • 45-minute deep-dive analysis using provided spreadsheet data
  • Deliverable: data-driven recommendations uploaded to Brightspace by one member
Analytical mindset Disentangle composite metrics before diagnosing. Low booking probability could be a CTR problem (ad) or a conversion problem (site) — they require completely different fixes.

How Ad Auctions Work
Real-time bidding · Second-price auction · Programmatic DSPs
Review ▼

Where Auctions Happen

PlatformTypeAuction Triggered By
Google AdsSearch + DisplayUser typing a query
Meta AdsFacebook/InstagramUser loading their feed
Amazon AdsProduct searchUser searching on Amazon
ProgrammaticAny websiteUser visiting a webpage

Google Search Auction — Step by Step

  1. User types a query → Google runs an instant auction among all bidders on that keyword
  2. Advertisers set a max bid: "I'll pay up to $3 per click for 'running shoes'"
  3. Google calculates Ad Rank = Max Bid × Quality Score
  4. Highest Ad Rank wins the top slot
  5. Winner pays only slightly more than the next bidder's price — second-price auction
Key insight You rarely pay your max bid. If you bid $3 but the next competitor's Ad Rank only justifies $1.50, you pay $1.51. This keeps bidding honest and prevents overbidding.

Programmatic Advertising

The entire auction process — across millions of websites — runs in ~100 milliseconds via Demand-Side Platforms (DSPs). Advertisers set rules; algorithms bid automatically on their behalf.

Market Definition Formula — The Key to Antitrust
Denominator manipulation · Sherman Act threshold · Prof. Ghose's core argument
Slide ▼
Market Share of Firm A
Market Shares of Firm A + B + C + D + ···
Fewer firms in the denominator → larger the market share of any given firm
The DOJ's strategy — shrink the denominator By excluding mobile app ads, native ads, social media ads, and video ads on news sites, the DOJ reduced the pool of competitors in the denominator. Smaller denominator = higher Google market share = more likely to cross the 70% monopoly threshold.
Google's defense — expand the denominator Include all substitutable ad formats: open web, social, retail media, streaming, gaming. Wider definition = Google at ~28% market share = far below the Sherman Act's 70% monopoly threshold.
Sherman Act monopoly threshold: 70%+ market share required → DOJ's narrow market: Google ~70%+ (claim) → Google's broad market: Google ~28% (actual)

Human vs. Machine Ad Buying
Ad sales teams vs real-time bidding algorithms · Tiffany & Co. NYT example
Slide ▼

Prof. Ghose used the NYT as the example publisher to contrast the two buying models side by side:

Old Way
Human — Ad Sales Teams

Tiffany & Co. calls the NYT ad sales team. They negotiate a rate card. Tiffany's banner runs on the homepage for a fixed CPM. One advertiser, one relationship, one placement.

New Way — RTB
Machines — Algorithms Evaluate Bids in Real-Time

When a user loads the NYT, an auction fires instantly. AT&T, Zipcar, Tiffany, Audi, Rolex, Gucci, T-Mobile all bid simultaneously. The highest bidder wins and their ad appears — in ~100ms.

Why this matters In the human model, Tiffany pays the same rate whether the reader is a 22-year-old student or a 55-year-old wealth manager. In the RTB model, Tiffany only bids high when the reader profile matches their target — and pays less (or nothing) for everyone else.

Real Example from the Slide

  • Same NYT article page: one reader sees a Tiffany & Co. leaderboard + rectangle (direct deal)
  • Another reader on the same page sees an AT&T GoPhone banner + a Zipcar rectangle (RTB-served, different audience profile)
  • Same URL, same content, different ads — the user determines the ad, not the page

Display vs. Search Engine Ads — The Halo Effect
Do display ads lift brand search behavior? · Randomized experiment design
Research ▼

The Research Question

  • What is the impact of display ads on search ads for a given brand?
  • What is the impact of display ads on consumer search and purchase behavior more broadly?
Why this is hard to answer People who see a display ad and then search for the brand might have searched anyway. You need a randomized experiment to isolate causality — just observing the correlation tells you nothing.

How You'd Design the Experiment

  • Randomly assign users into two groups: treatment (sees brand display ad) and control (sees an unrelated ad)
  • Measure subsequent search behavior for the advertised brand across both groups
  • The difference in search rates = causal effect of the display ad
  • Key: the control ad must be from a completely different category so it doesn't influence brand searches

MMM Extensions — Saturation, Interaction & Lag
Quadratic terms · Omnichannel synergies · Time-delayed effects
Key ▼

A basic MMM regresses sales on ad spend by channel. But three extensions make it dramatically more accurate:

1 — Saturation Effects (Diminishing Returns)

  • First dollar of ad spend → high return
  • Hundredth dollar → near-zero return
  • Modeled with a quadratic (squared) term on spend
  • Tells you exactly where the curve flattens — your budget ceiling
Sales = α + β₁·Spend + β₂·Spend² + ε

β₂ < 0 captures the diminishing return

2 — Interaction Effects (Omnichannel Synergies)

  • TV + Search together outperform TV alone + Search alone added up
  • Display ad exposure → lifts branded search CTR (the Yahoo! halo we studied)
  • Captured by an interaction term: TV_Spend × Search_Spend
  • Without this term, MMM underestimates top-of-funnel channels because their value flows through lower-funnel channels
Sales = α + β₁·TV + β₂·Search + β₃·(TV × Search) + ε β₃ > 0 = synergy exists between the two channels

3 — Lag Effects (Time-Delayed Impact)

  • A TV ad run in October may drive sales in November and December
  • Display ads build brand memory that converts weeks later
  • Modeled by including lagged spend variables: Spendt-1, Spendt-2, etc.
  • Without lag terms, MMM underattributes brand-building channels and overattributes performance channels
Why these three matter together Without saturation → you'll overbid on channels past their effective ceiling. Without interaction → you'll cut TV thinking it has low ROI (missing that it amplifies search). Without lag → you'll underinvest in brand-building because the payoff is delayed. All three biases push companies toward short-term performance marketing and away from brand investment.

Why landing page quality was insignificant — then suddenly significant Walmart had a uniquely good landing page for a specific product. But competitors noticed and immediately copied the design. Count Data Models — Poisson & NBREG
🔢
When Y is a Count — Model Selection Framework
OLS failure · Logit vs Poisson vs NBREG · Overdispersion test
Key ▼

The Core Decision Tree

What type is your dependent variable (Y)?
Binary (0 or 1)
Logit
Sale occurred? Yes/No. Outputs probability 0–1.
Count, mean ≈ variance
Poisson
Clicks, sessions, # purchases. Equidispersion.
Count, variance >> mean
NBREG
Overdispersed count data — the real-world default.

Why Not OLS for Binary/Count Y?

  • OLS: Y = a + bX → unbounded, can predict 1.3 or −0.2 (impossible probabilities)
  • Logit uses the logistic S-curve: P(Y=1) = 1 / (1 + e^−z) → always between 0 and 1
  • Logit coefficients are log-odds — convert via e^coefficient to get odds ratios

Poisson vs NBREG

PoissonNegative Binomial (NBREG)
AssumptionMean = VarianceVariance > Mean (overdispersion)
Real-world fitRare — data rarely equidispersedCommon default for count data
Extra parameterNoneAlpha (dispersion parameter)
STATA commandpoisson Y Xnbreg Y X
Test for overdispersion in STATA Run nbreg Y X — if the alpha parameter is statistically significant, your data is overdispersed → use NBREG, not Poisson. Significance results can differ between models when overdispersion is present.

Interpreting Coefficients

  • Take e^(coefficient) to get the incidence rate ratio (IRR)
  • Example: coefficient = 0.676 → e^0.676 = 1.97 → one-unit increase in AverageRating nearly doubles the odds of a sale

Model Selection
Q1 When should you use Logit vs Poisson vs NBREG? What is the decision rule? ▼
Check your dependent variable Y. If Y is binary (0/1) → Logit. If Y is a count and mean ≈ variance → Poisson. If Y is a count and variance >> mean (overdispersion) → NBREG. In STATA, run nbreg and check if alpha is significant — significant alpha = use NBREG.
Q2 Why can't you use OLS when Y is binary? ▼
OLS is unbounded — it can predict values like 1.3 or −0.2, which are impossible probabilities. Logit uses the logistic S-curve P(Y=1) = 1/(1+e^−z) which constrains all predictions between 0 and 1.
Q3 How do you interpret a logit coefficient? ▼
Logit coefficients are log-odds. To interpret: take e^(coefficient). If coefficient = 0.676, then e^0.676 = 1.97, meaning a one-unit increase in that variable nearly doubles the odds of the outcome occurring.

Digital Advertising & Day 3 Topics
Q12 What does AIDA stand for and what does it describe? ▼
Awareness → Interest → Desire → Action. It maps the consumer purchase journey from first exposure to final conversion. Digital marketing channels are mapped to different stages: display ads build awareness; search ads capture intent (Desire/Action stage).
Q13 What is the key insight from the Yahoo! display advertising experiment? ▼
Display ads have a halo effect on search. Users exposed to display ads from a brand subsequently searched for that brand more often, even without clicking the display ad. Implication: measuring display ROI by clicks alone understates its true value — it also lifts branded search volume.
Q14 What is the difference between CPC and CPM? Which is better for which objective? ▼
CPC (Cost-per-Click): you pay only when someone clicks — best for performance/conversion goals. CPM (Cost-per-Mille): you pay per 1,000 impressions regardless of clicks — best for awareness/branding goals. Key: match the pricing model to the campaign objective.
Q15 In a Google ad auction, how is ad rank calculated and what does it mean? ▼
Ad Rank = Bid × Quality Score. Quality Score accounts for ad relevance, expected CTR, and landing page experience. This means a lower bidder with a higher Quality Score can outrank a higher bidder. Google uses a second-price auction: the winner pays just enough to beat the next-highest ad rank, not their full bid.
Q16 What is RTB (Real-Time Bidding) and how does it work? ▼
RTB is programmatic ad buying where impressions are auctioned in real time (~100ms) as a webpage loads. Publisher sends impression data to an SSP → Ad Exchange → DSPs bid on behalf of advertisers → highest bid wins → ad is served. Replaced manual insertion orders, enabling precise audience targeting at scale.
Q17 What is the central argument in the Google antitrust case and why does market definition matter? ▼
DOJ argued Google illegally monopolized the search advertising market. Market definition is the battleground: if you define the market as "all digital advertising," Google has ~25% share (not a monopoly). If you define it as "general search advertising," Google has ~90% share (clearly a monopoly). Prof. Ghose served as Google's expert witness arguing for the broader market definition.
Q18 What is a Marketing Mix Model (MMM) and what is the "substitutability" argument? ▼
MMM is a statistical model (typically OLS) that attributes sales to different marketing channels (TV, digital, social, etc.) using aggregate time-series data. The substitutability argument (Ghose's expert testimony): if consumers treat Google search and Bing/DuckDuckGo as substitutes, Google's market power is constrained — supporting a broad market definition and weakening the monopoly claim.

T/F 6 Display ads can increase branded search volume even when users don't click on them. ▼
✓ TRUE. This is the halo effect shown in the Yahoo! experiment. Display ad exposure raised brand salience — users later searched for the brand organically. Measuring display ROI by clicks alone severely underestimates its value.
T/F 7 The Google antitrust case outcome depends heavily on how broadly the relevant market is defined. ▼
✓ TRUE. Broad market = Google has ~25% share (competitive). Narrow market (general search ads) = Google has ~90% share (monopoly). Prof. Ghose testified for Google arguing the broad definition. Market definition is the entire game in antitrust cases.
T/F 8 Negative reviews always hurt a product's sales. ▼
✗ FALSE. Negative reviews can INCREASE sales when they are informative and specific. Example: "video problems with Kingston SDHC cards" — a buyer who doesn't use Kingston reads this as a green light. Vague negative reviews hurt; specific negative reviews can segment and help.
T/F 9 The Social Listening Model contains 5 independent variables. ▼
✗ FALSE. The model has 9 variables: Avg. Rating, Volume, Subjectivity, Spelling Errors, Grammatical Errors, % Helpful Votes, Reviewer Identity Disclosure, Avg. Readability, Sentiment-Feature pairs.
T/F 10 The relationship between review volume and sales is linear — each additional review adds the same sales lift. ▼
✗ FALSE. The relationship is concave (diminishing returns). First review = +10% lift. By 50 reviews = +30% total. Going from 50 to 100 = only +7% more. Similar to advertising saturation — biggest returns come from the first few reviews.
T/F 11 Spelling and grammar quality in online content has measurable economic consequences. ▼
✓ TRUE. Multiple studies confirm: Zappos fixed 6M reviews → +7% sales. Hotel listings with grammar errors → 13% demand drop. Real estate listings without errors → sold 3 days faster and 10× more likely to sell. Dating profiles → 14% higher match rate.
T/F 12 "Good packaging" in a product review is a positive signal that increases price premium. ▼
✗ FALSE. "Good packaging" is damning with faint praise — coefficient is −0.56% on price premium. The fact that a reviewer mentions basic packaging suggests there's little else to praise. Context matters: people interpret language by what's implied, not just what's said.
T/F 13 Text mining of online discussions is a leading indicator of brand perception shifts, while sales data is lagging. ▼
✓ TRUE. The Cadillac case demonstrates this. Forum discussions on Edmunds.com showed Cadillac's repositioning working before it showed up in sales. Text = leading indicator. Sales = lagging indicator. This is the strategic value of social listening.
T/F 14 Lift > 1 between two brands means they are mentioned together less often than expected by chance. ▼
✗ FALSE. Lift > 1 means brands are mentioned together more often than chance would predict — they are perceived as close substitutes. lift(A,B) = P(A,B) / [P(A)×P(B)]. Lift < 1 would mean they're mentioned together less than expected.
T/F 15 Poisson regression assumes that the variance of Y equals the mean of Y. ▼
✓ TRUE. This is the equidispersion assumption. In reality, count data is almost always overdispersed (variance > mean), which is why NBREG is typically preferred over Poisson for real-world data.
T/F 16 If the alpha parameter in NBREG is not statistically significant, Poisson is the better model choice. ▼
✓ TRUE. Non-significant alpha means the overdispersion parameter is indistinguishable from zero — your data is equidispersed, meaning Poisson's mean=variance assumption holds. Use Poisson. Significant alpha → use NBREG.
T/F 17 NBREG and Poisson coefficients are interpreted the same way as OLS coefficients. ▼
✗ FALSE. You must exponentiate: e^(coefficient) gives you the incidence rate ratio. Example: coefficient = 0.676 → e^0.676 = 1.97 → one-unit increase in that variable nearly doubles the expected count. Direct coefficient = log of the rate ratio, not the rate itself.
T/F 18 Crowded environments reduce mobile purchase rates because people are distracted and uncomfortable. ▼
✗ FALSE. Counter-intuitive empirical finding: crowdedness increases purchase rates from 2.73% to 4.04% — a +48.1% lift. Possible explanation: crowded environments create social proof and urgency. Marketers should target users in crowded contexts, not avoid them.
T/F 19 Trajectory-based mobile targeting (where the user is heading) outperforms location-only targeting (where they are now). ▼
✓ TRUE. Trajectory targeting was the best-performing type in the experiment (83,370 users). Knowing where someone is going is more predictive than knowing where they are now. The TAP framework emphasizes targeting the full movement context, not just the snapshot.
T/F 20 In the social listening model, a reviewer disclosing their real identity (vs. anonymous) has no effect on the credibility or impact of the review. ▼
✗ FALSE. Reviewer Identity Disclosure is one of the 9 variables in the Social Listening Model — it has a statistically significant effect. Disclosed-identity reviewers are perceived as more credible, affecting both the weight the model gives the review and consumer interpretation of sentiment.

SA 1 A STATA output shows the coefficient for ReviewVolume is 0.046 and significant at p<0.01. Interpret this and state the business implication. ▼
Interpretation: A one-unit increase in ReviewVolume is associated with a 0.046-unit increase in the log-odds of a sale (if logit) or a 4.6% increase in expected sales count (if count model, after exponentiation). The coefficient is statistically significant — we can rule out chance with 99% confidence.

Business implication: Generating more reviews drives sales. However, given the concave relationship in the literature, the marginal return per additional review declines — early reviews matter most. Prioritize review acquisition for new products with zero reviews before optimizing further.
SA 2 You run NBREG in STATA and the alpha parameter is not significant (p = 0.43). What does this tell you? What should you do next? ▼
What it tells you: Alpha (the dispersion parameter) is not statistically distinguishable from zero. This means your count data does not exhibit overdispersion — variance ≈ mean, and Poisson's equidispersion assumption holds.

What to do: Rerun the model using poisson Y X. Poisson is the appropriate model when variance ≈ mean. Using NBREG when it's unnecessary adds an estimated parameter without improving fit — violates parsimony.
SA 3 Explain why text mining of online forums is strategically valuable for Cadillac's brand management, and what the Edmunds.com case demonstrated. ▼
Strategic value: Text mining captures unsolicited consumer opinions at scale — what people actually think, not what they say when asked. It reveals brand perceptions, competitive positioning, and feature-level sentiment without the cost and bias of surveys.

Edmunds.com case: By building a co-occurrence (lift) matrix from forum discussions, researchers created a perceptual map of the used-car market. The map correlated 0.76 with JD Power PIN transaction data (actual revealed-preference purchases). Key insight: the text mining map was a leading indicator — it detected Cadillac's successful repositioning as a performance brand before it appeared in sales data. Managers can use this for early-stage strategy evaluation without waiting for financial results.
